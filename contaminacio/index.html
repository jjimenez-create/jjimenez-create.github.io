Air pollution, meteorological conditions and respiratory infections in Baix Llobregat. A 14-year spatiotemporal analysis

The objective of this research project are:

  • Joaquín & Didac are studyng all data from Viladecans that is a city located in Baix Llobregat county. In this county we are using data from Air Pollution Prevention and Monitoring Network (XPVCA in Catalan) corresponding to hourly data of air pollutants from year 1991 to 2025. Data included: benzene (C6H6), carbon monoxide (CO), nitric oxide (NO), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), ozone (O3), sulfur dioxide (SO2)
  • Meteorological data is available from Meteocat XEMA (Meteorological data server of Catalonia) , so we choose the nearest meteorological station that is Vallirana. The data structure is based on semi-hourly data that is, data every 30 minutes from 2011. Data inclouded: temperature, relative humidity, pressure, wind direction, wind speed, solar irradiance
  • Respiratory infection data are collected from the Information System for Infection Surveillance in Catalonia (SIVIC) using data from region 75, which is the Barcelona Metropolitan South region; 265 is the Primary Care Centre from Viladecans.

    • 1. Area of study

    For this project, each group was assigned a different locality within Catalonia. Our group's study focuses on El Prat (Jardins de la Pau), located in the Barcelona Metropolitan South area, region 75 of the SIVIC data.

    El Prat de Llobregat is a city of approximately 66,000 inhabitants, located in the Baix Llobregat region and part of the Barcelona Metropolitan Area. Its strategic location is only 2–3 km from Barcelona–El Prat Airport, which implies a significant influence of air traffic on air quality. The city is also connected by highways, trains, and metro, acting as a logistics and industrial corridor with nearby industrial areas and port and railway activities.

    All of this, together with heavy urban traffic, constitutes the main sources of air pollution that may affect the respiratory health of the population.

    2. Data sources

    • SIVIC (Health data): The Information System for Infection Surveillance in Catalonia (SIVIC) is a surveillance system created by the Catalan Public Health Agency to monitor respiratory infections such as Covid‑19, influenza, respiratory syncytial virus, and enteroviruses. It integrates data collected from primary care centres, hospitals, laboratories and public health services, allowing real‑time analysis of acute respiratory infections to detect trends and provide early alerts.
    • XEMA (Meteorological data): The Network of Automatic Meteorological Stations managed by Meteocat includes more than 180 stations measuring temperature, precipitation, wind, humidity, and pressure in real time.
    • XPVCA (Air pollution data): Catalonia’s official air quality monitoring network that provides real-time pollutant data to study air quality trends and environmental health impacts.

    4. Results

    • NO2: European legislation establishes that nitrogen dioxide should not exceed 200 µg/m³ more than 18 times per year. In our analysis (2009–2025), we observed 0 exceedances of this hourly limit. However, the annual limit was exceeded in 2 of the 16 years analyzed (12.5% of the period).

    5. Discussion

    Most influential pollutants

    The most relevant pollutant is nitrogen dioxide (NO2). It is the only pollutant exceeding the annual limit in some years and shows frequent daily peaks related to traffic and urban activity. Ozone (O3) and sulfur dioxide (SO2) do not show significant exceedances.

    Meteorological conditions affecting pollution

    Solar radiation favors ozone formation, especially during midday. Wind helps disperse pollutants, while calm conditions favor accumulation. During winter, thermal inversions may trap pollutants near the ground, increasing NO2.

    Seasonal patterns

    NO2 concentrations show two daily peaks coinciding with rush hours. Levels are usually higher on weekdays than weekends. Seasonally, NO2 increases in winter, while ozone levels are higher in summer due to stronger solar radiation.

    Public health implications

    Even when legal limits are generally met, NO2 exposure may still affect health. It is associated with respiratory irritation, asthma aggravation, and other respiratory problems, especially in vulnerable populations such as children, the elderly, and people with chronic diseases.
